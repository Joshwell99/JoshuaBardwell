Intro

My name is Joshua Bardwell, 2nd year astronomy graduate student at San Diego State University. My interests in astronomy first began during my undergrad when I interned for the James Webb Space Telescope under the Northrop Grumman Executive team. In my fourth year of undergrad at CalPoly San Luis Obispo I proceeded to do research in Brightest Cluster Galaxy mergers and morphology, which soldified my passion in astronomy and astrophysics. In the summer prior to attending graduate school I had the pleasure to intern for NASA Goddard Space Flight center where I developed DARMA a computer program which analyzes complex spectra and determines their molecular compositions.

In addition to my love for science and computer programming, I have a strong passion for clothing and logo design! At my first internship for the JWST I spent the first half of my session designing NASA T-shirts to promote the JWST launch. Unfortunately, these designs are protected under an NDA. However, please refer to the creative works section to see all my other astronomy related designs!

Scientific Works

Black holes are arguably the most enigmatic and fascinating objects we know of in the universe. A region of spacetime with so much mass that its escape velocity is greater than the speed of light, creating a cosmic cloak which inhibits any sentient observers from obtaining any information beyond the object's mass and spin. And what's better than one black hole? How about two black holes in binary with one another, ending in an inevitable merger event? One stellar-mass black hole and another supermassive blackhole in this configuration is denoted as an extreme-mass ratio inspiral (EMRI). Consequences of General Relativity tell us that energy is radiated away from this inspiral in the form of gravitational waves. My master's thesis focuses on developing the gravitational wave signal templates for a future space mission called LISA!

Roughly 5 billion years ago our solar system was a very different place then it is today. Primarily consisting of a localized cloud of hydrogen and helium gas reduced to a protoplanetary disk, our host star (the Sun) formed at the center of this gravitational well. As time progressed, elastic collisions between molecules allowed for the formation of planets and the radiation pressure of our Sun slowly blew this gas to outer portions of our solar neighborhood. The outer portions were in regimes that allowed for the continuing existence of ices consisting of hydrogen, helium, carbon, and oxygen. The orbital disruption of these objects causes them to fall into eccentric orbits about the Sun, sublimating their gas, creating a spectacle which we call comets. I worked with spectra of these experimentally created comets which have been incinerated by a ultraviolet laser. I know... I can't believe it either! These comets were made in a lab at NASA Goddard called SubLIME. While working for this lab, I developed a program called DARMA (Detection Automation of Rotational-peaks and Molecular Assignment). This automated-program is able to determine the molecular composition of the gaseous byproducts which sublimate off these cometary nucleus. Understanding what molecules are made from sublimated comets helps us understand the source of planetary atmospheres (presummed to be constructed partially by in-falling comets) including our own.

The universe tends towards a heiarchal structure of organization from the microscopic realm of atoms and molecules to the macroscopic regime of interstellar space. In extragalactic terms, atoms make hydrogen and helium, hydrogen and helium make stars, stars make galaxies, and galaxies feed the growth of Brightest Cluster Galaxies?? Brightest Cluster Galaxies, or BCGs, are the largest known galaxies in the local universe. The growth and morphology of these unfathomably large objects strictly depends on the cannabilzation of smaller galaxies within its vicinty. For my undergraduate thesis I researched the evolution of BCG's and their contribution to the Interstellar Medium.

Creative Works

DARMA, Detection Automation of Rotational peaks and Molecular Assignment, is a pythonic program which takes a spectra full of rotational transitions, finds its peaks, and determines the molecules which those peaks belong to. This was a logo I developed for the program. See Github for the full repository.

SubLIME, Sublimation of Laboratory Ices Millimeter submilimeter Experiment, is a laboratory at NASA Goddard code 691 (Astrobiology). I developed this logo while working for SubLIME, where I developed DARMA. If you go on a tour of Goddard and stop at SubLIME, you may recieved a sticker or t-shirt with my logo on it!

JWST TSS, James Webb Space Telescope Team Solar System, is a coalition of scientists within NASA Goddard that focuses on projects which advances solar system science with JWST data. I developed many promotional items for them (e.g. logos, t-shirt designs), one of which was an iron-on patch!

Outreach

'It is the supreme art of the teacher to awaken joy in creative expression and knowledge' - Albert Einstein

Although, participating in discovery through science is an amazing experience, communicating science to the public is one of my most favorite activities. In this image I am giving a talk on An Introduction to Astronomy and the Night Sky at my master program's observatory, Mount Laguna Observatory. To amplify the learning experience I like to maximize audience engagement. To attain this goal I provide astronomy trivia questions throughout the lecture, which audience members can answer and possibly win prizes (e.g. NASA posters, solar eclipse glasses, and space stickers)!

Within STEM, I have found a strong need to connect students with the amazing scientific applications of skills obtained in introductory technical classes such as Chemistry, Calculus, or Physics. For example, within this spectroscopy lab I first teach the fundamentals so that I can explain its applications to understanding the compositions of astronomical objects like exoplanet atmospheres and comets!

Astronomy is a discipline which is noted for its technical feats and rich history. Keeping up with this wide scope is incredibly important when communicating with the public at Star Parties.
